Metacognition broadly refers to the ability to accurately reflect on one's own mental states, and is important for adaptive behaviour because it enables us to identify our errors and adapt our behaviour according to our uncertainty about our inferences. Mentalising (theory of mind) - the ability to infer the mental states of others - is also important for adaptive behaviour for obvious reasons: successful interactions with others requires us to have at least some understanding of their beliefs and knowledge, or lack thereof ("I changed my plans and am no longer free for dinner - I should let my housemate know.").
Recent work (1,2) has proposed that mentalising can be described in terms of hierarchical bayesian inference, where the "sophistication" of a person's mentalising ability is the degree of recursive inference they can perform (e.g. "you think I think that the milk has gone off").
The goal of this project is to take this model and adapt it to (pre-existing) human data from simple metacognitive tasks to test it against human performance on simple metacognitive tasks.

Metacognition is the ability reflect on one's internal states, and this includes the ability to evaluate the accuracy or efficacy of our own decisions or inferences: do we know when we made a good versus bad choice? In the lab, metacognition in this sense is often measured by using simple (and dull) tasks such as discriminating the motion direction of a dot cloud, collecting subjective confidence ratings with each decision, and looking for correspondences between confidence and choice accuracy.

In the board game Confident?, players answer trivia questions such as "How many hours a week does the average Brit watch Netflix?" by reporting a range of values they think the answer lies in. The round is won by the player who reports the narrowest range that includes the correct answer. There are also bonus cards which can occasionally be used, which allow you to either swap your answer with another player, or to double the points you will receive.

This game is an interesting toy model for metacognition in group settings: players need to evaluate the uncertainty about our inferences, but they also learn about the knowledge/performance of others and have the option to defer responses to them (the switch cards). Therefore, reward maximisation in this game requires learning over iterations both one's own metacognitive ability, and that of one's opponents.
